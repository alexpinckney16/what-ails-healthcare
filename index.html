The price of the white coat

Three students walk into the same medical school, match into the same specialty, and pay the same share of their income toward loans. The only difference is what they owed at 22. This model shows what that difference costs — and when, if ever, each of them gets out.

Methodology and sources

This is a stylized model, not financial advice. Assumptions:

  • All three personas graduate college at 22 differing only in education debt: $0; $30,000 at 6.5%; or $70,000 at 7.5% (higher rate reflecting private and Parent PLUS borrowing common among lower-wealth families).
  • Medical school runs ages 22–26 with equal annual borrowing (adjustable) at the blended grad loan rate (adjustable; the 2025–26 federal rates were 7.94% unsubsidized and 8.94% Grad PLUS).
  • Residency begins at 26 with length set by specialty. Loan interest accrues throughout with no pause; payments are the chosen share of after-tax residency pay (a 22% effective tax rate is assumed for residents).
  • Attending pay begins when residency ends. Effective tax rates by specialty income: 34% (pediatrics, primary care), 36% (psychiatry), 38% (general surgery), 40% (orthopedics) — approximate single-filer federal + FICA + average state.
  • Payments are applied to the higher-rate loans first. Interest compounds monthly throughout, a simplification that slightly overstates balances versus federal simple-interest accrual before capitalization; the six-month grace period and income-driven-repayment interest subsidies are ignored.
  • Salary figures are rounded blends of 2025 physician compensation surveys (AMA, Doximity, Medscape): orthopedic surgery ~$580K, general surgery ~$450K, psychiatry ~$340K, primary care ~$275K, pediatrics ~$260K. Median PGY-1 stipend was $66,986 (AAMC, 2025, preliminary).
  • Debt statistics informing defaults: AAMC Education Debt Fact Card (Class of 2025) and AAMC Graduation Questionnaire.
